Fundamentals of Robotic Actuation
Actuation converts electrical, hydraulic, or pneumatic energy into mechanical motion. The choice of actuation technology fundamentally shapes a robot's capabilities, from the forces it can exert to its speed, precision, and energy efficiency. Understanding the principles, advantages, and limitations of different actuation approaches is essential for designing effective Physical AI systems.

Electric Actuation Systems
DC Motors and Brushless Motors
Direct current (DC) motors are the workhorses of modern robotics, converting electrical energy into rotational motion through electromagnetic interactions. Brushed DC motors use mechanical commutation—brushes that switch current direction as the rotor rotates. They offer simple control interfaces and predictable torque-speed characteristics but suffer from wear and electromagnetic interference from brush arcing.
Brushless DC (BLDC) motors replace mechanical commutation with electronic switching, eliminating brushes entirely. This design offers higher efficiency, longer lifetimes, and better power-to-weight ratios. However, BLDC motors require more sophisticated electronic controllers that synchronize phase currents with rotor position, necessitating position sensors or sensorless estimation algorithms.
Motor selection involves balancing multiple specifications. Continuous torque determines sustained load capacity, while peak torque defines maximum momentary capability. Speed ratings indicate rotational velocity limits. Motor constants (Kt and Ke) characterize torque production per ampere and back-EMF per radian per second respectively, relating electrical and mechanical domains.
Servo Motors and Stepper Motors
Servo motors integrate position feedback into the motor package, creating closed-loop position control systems. These units combine a motor, gearbox, position sensor, and control electronics in a compact package. Hobby servos provide simple, affordable position control for applications requiring moderate precision and torque.
Industrial servo systems offer higher performance with absolute encoders, field-oriented control, and sophisticated trajectory generation. These systems can track rapidly changing position commands while rejecting disturbances, making them ideal for precision manipulation and manufacturing applications.
Stepper motors move in discrete angular increments (steps) in response to pulse commands. Operating in open-loop mode without position feedback, steppers provide acceptable positioning for many applications at lower cost than servo systems. However, they can lose steps under excessive load or rapid acceleration, and they consume power even when stationary to maintain holding torque.
Transmission Systems and Gearing
Transmission systems modify the speed and torque characteristics of actuators to match application requirements. Gear reduction trades speed for torque, enabling high-speed motors to produce the large forces needed for manipulation and locomotion. Common transmission types include spur gears, planetary gears, harmonic drives, and belt drives, each with distinct efficiency, backlash, and packaging characteristics.
Backlash—the angular play in a transmission when reversing direction—affects positioning accuracy and control stability. Precision applications require transmissions with minimal backlash, often achieved through preloaded designs or backlash-free mechanisms like harmonic drives. However, reduced backlash often comes with increased friction and cost.
Gear ratios fundamentally alter the actuator's effective characteristics. A 100:1 reduction increases torque by a factor of 100 while decreasing speed by the same factor (neglecting efficiency losses). The reflected inertia seen at the motor scales with the square of the gear ratio, affecting dynamic response and control design.
Hydraulic and Pneumatic Systems
Hydraulic Actuation Principles
Hydraulic systems use pressurized fluid to generate large forces in compact packages. The incompressibility of hydraulic fluid enables precise position control and high stiffness. Hydraulic actuators achieve remarkable power-to-weight ratios, making them ideal for applications requiring substantial force, such as legged locomotion in large robots or heavy manipulation tasks.
Hydraulic systems comprise pumps that pressurize fluid, valves that direct flow to actuators, and the hydraulic cylinders or motors that produce motion. Proportional and servo valves enable closed-loop control by modulating fluid flow in response to electrical commands. Accumulators store energy and smooth pressure fluctuations.
Despite their advantages, hydraulic systems present significant challenges. They require pumps, reservoirs, and potentially thermal management. Fluid leaks pose environmental and maintenance concerns. Achieving smooth, precise motion requires sophisticated valve control to overcome friction and compensate for fluid compressibility effects at high pressures.
